\section{Background on Coalgebra and GKAT}

\subsection{Concepts in Universal Coalgebra}

In this paper, we will make heavy use of coalgebraic theory, thus it is empirical for us to recall some notions and useful theorems in universal coalgebra.
Given a functor \(F\) on the category of set and functions, a \emph{coalgebra over \(F\)} or \emph{\(F\)-coalgebra} consists of a set \(S\) and a function \(œÉ_S: S ‚Üí F(S)\).
We typically call elements in \(S\) the \emph{states} of the coalgebra, and \(œÉ_S(s)\) the \emph{dynamic} of state \(s\).
We will sometimes use the states \(S\) to denote the coalgebra, when no ambiguity can arise. 

A homomorphism between two \(F\)-coalgebra \(S\) and \(U\) is a map \(h: S ‚Üí U\) that preserves the function \(œÉ\); diagrammatically, the following diagram commutes:
\[
    \begin{tikzcd}
        S \ar{r}{h} \ar[swap]{d}{œÉ_S} & U \ar{d}{œÉ_U} \\  
        F(S) \ar{r}{F(h)} & F(U)
    \end{tikzcd}    
\]

When we can restrict the homomorphism map into a inclusion map \(i: S' ‚Üí S\) for \(S' ‚äÜ S\), then we say that \(S'\) is a \emph{sub-coalgebra} of \(S\), denoted as \(S' ‚äë S\). Specifically, the following diagram commutes when \(S' ‚äë S\):
\[
    \begin{tikzcd}
        S' \ar[hook]{r}{i} \ar[swap]{d}{œÉ_{S'}} & S \ar{d}{œÉ_S} \\  
        F(S') \ar[hook]{r}{F(i)} & F(S)
    \end{tikzcd}    
\]
In fact, the function \(œÉ_{S'}\) is uniquely determined by the states \(S'\)~\cite[Proposition 6.1]{rutten_UniversalCoalgebraTheory_2000}.

The sub-coalgebras are preserved under homomorphic images and pre-images: 
\begin{lemma}[Theorem 6.3~\cite{rutten_UniversalCoalgebraTheory_2000}]\label{thm:hom-(pre)img-preserve-sub-coalg}
    given a homomorphism \(h: S ‚Üí U\), and sub-coalgebras \(S' ‚äë S\) and \(U' ‚äë U\), then 
    \[h(S') ‚äë U \text{ and } h^{-1}(U') ‚äë S.\]
\end{lemma}

One particularly important sub-coalgebra of and coalgebra \(S\) is the least coalgebra generated by a single element \(s\). 
We will denote this sub-coalgebra as \(‚ü®s‚ü©_{S}\), and call it \emph{principle sub-coalgebra} generated by \(s\). 
We sometimes omit the subscript \(S\) when it can be inferred from context or irrelevant.
Intuitively, we usually think of principle sub-coalgebra \(‚ü®s‚ü©_S\) as the sub-coalgebra that is formed by all the ``reachable state'' form the state \(s\).
This coalgebraic characterization of reachable state can allow us to avoid induction on the length of path from \(s\) to reach another state.

For all coalgebra \(S\) and a state \(s ‚àà S\), principle sub-coalgebra \(‚ü®s‚ü©_S\) always exists and is unique, because sub-coalgebra of any coalgebra forms a complete lattice~\cite[theorem 6.4]{rutten_UniversalCoalgebraTheory_2000}; thus taking the meet of all the sub-coalgebra that contains \(s\) will yield \(‚ü®s‚ü©_S\).

Similar to sub-coalgebra, principle sub-coalgebra is also preserved under homomorphic image:
\begin{theorem}\label{thm:homo-img-preserve-principle-sub-coalg}
    Homomorphic image preserves principle sub-GKAT coalgebra. Specifically, given a homomorphism \(h: S ‚Üí U\):
    \[h(‚ü®s‚ü©_S) = ‚ü®h(s)‚ü©_U\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    We will need to show that \(h(‚ü®s‚ü©_{S})\) is the smallest sub-GKAT coalgebra of \(S\) that contain \(h(s)\). First by definition of image, \(h(s) ‚àà h(‚ü®s‚ü©_{S})\); second by \cref{thm:hom-(pre)img-preserve-sub-coalg}, \(h(‚ü®S‚ü©_S) ‚äë U\).

    Finally, take any \(U' ‚äë U\) and \(h(s) ‚àà U'\), recall that by \cref{thm:hom-(pre)img-preserve-sub-coalg}, \(h^{-1}(U') ‚äë S\). We can then derive that \(h(‚ü®s‚ü©_S) ‚äë U'\): 
    \begin{align*}
        h(s) ‚àà U' 
        & ‚üπ s ‚àà h^{-1}(U') \\  
        & ‚üπ ‚ü®s‚ü©_S ‚äë h^{-1}(U') & \text{definition of \(‚ü®s‚ü©_S\)}\\  
        & ‚üπ h(‚ü®s‚ü©_S) ‚äë U' & \text{\cref{thm:hom-(pre)img-preserve-sub-coalg}}
    \end{align*}
    Hence \(h(‚ü®s‚ü©_S)\) is the smallest sub-GKAT coalgebra of \(U\) that contains \(h(s)\).
\end{proof}

% bisimulation

A \emph{final coalgebra} \(‚Ñ±\) over a signature \(F\), sometimes called the \emph{behavior} of coalgebras over \(F\), is a \(F\)-coalgebra s.t. for all \(F\)-coalgebra \(S\), there exists a unique homomorphism \(‚ü¶-‚üß_S: S ‚Üí ‚Ñ±\).

Given two \(F\)-coalgebra \(S\) and \(U\), the \emph{behavioral equivalence} between states in \(S\) and \(U\) can be computed by a notion called \emph{bisimulation}.
A relation \({‚àº} ‚äÜ S √ó U\) is called a \emph{bisimulation relation} if it forms an \(F\)-coalgebra: \[œÉ_{‚àº}: {‚àº} ‚Üí F(‚àº),\] 
And its projections \(œÄ‚ÇÅ: S √ó U ‚Üí S\) and \(œÄ‚ÇÇ: S √ó U ‚Üí U\) are both homomorphisms:
\[
    \begin{tikzcd}
        S \ar[swap]{d}{œÉ_S}
            & ‚àº \ar[swap]{l}{œÄ‚ÇÅ} \ar{r}{œÄ‚ÇÇ} \ar{d}{œÉ_{‚àº}} 
            & U \ar{d}{œÉ_T}\\  
        F(S) & F(‚àº) \ar{l}{F(œÄ‚ÇÅ)} \ar[swap]{r}{F(œÄ‚ÇÇ)} & F(T)
    \end{tikzcd}
\]
In a special case, when there exists a homomorphism \(h: S ‚Üí U\), then we can simply pick \({‚àº} ‚äÜ S √ó U\) to be \(\{(s, h(s)) ‚à£ s ‚àà S\}\), which gives us a bisimulation with the lift \(œÉ_{‚àº} ‚âú œÉ_S √ó œÉ_U\).

\begin{corollary}
    Given two \(F\)-coalgebra \(S, U\) and a homomorphism \(h: S ‚Üí U\), then all for all \(s\), \(‚ü¶s‚üß_{S} = ‚ü¶h(s)‚üß_{U}.\)
\end{corollary}

Finally, we are interested in bisimulation equivalence.
Given a coalgebra \(S\), a bisimulation \({‚âÉ} ‚äÜ S √ó S\) is a bisimulation equivalence when \(‚âÉ\) is a bisimulation and also an equivalence relation.
As it turns out, searching for bisimulation is equivalent to bisimulation equivalence. 
This result is very important, as it allows us to leverage more efficient data structure, like union-find, to search for equivalence relation instead of relations in general.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:bisim-iff-bisim-eq}
    For any coalgebra \(S\), there exists a bisimulation \({‚àº} ‚äÜ S √ó S\) s.t. \(s‚ÇÅ ‚àº s‚ÇÇ\) if and only if there exists a bisimulation equivalence \({‚âÉ} ‚äÜ S √ó S\) s.t. \(s‚ÇÅ ‚âÉ s‚ÇÇ\)
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    First show the \(‚üπ\) direction, we consider the maximal bisimulation between \(S\) and itself \({‚â°} ‚äÜ S √ó S\), which is known to be a bisimulation equivalence~\cite[Corollary 5.6]{rutten_UniversalCoalgebraTheory_2000}.
    Because \(‚â°\) is maximal, therefore \({‚àº} ‚äÜ {‚â°}\) and \(s‚ÇÅ ‚â° s‚ÇÇ\).

    Then the \(‚ü∏\) direction can be proven by setting \({‚àº} ‚âú {‚âÉ}\).
\end{proof}

\subsection{GKAT and Its Coalgebra}

Guarded Kleene Algebra with Tests, or GKAT~\cite{smolka_GuardedKleeneAlgebra_2020}, is a deterministic fragment of Kleene Algebra with Tests. 
The syntax of GKAT over a set of primitive actions \(K\) and a set of primitive tests \(T\) can be defined in two sorts, boolean expressions \(\Bool\) and expressions \(\Exp\):
\begin{align*}
    a, b, c ‚àà \Bool 
        & ‚âú 1 ‚à£ 0 ‚à£ t ‚àà T ‚à£ b ‚àß c ‚à£ b ‚à® c ‚à£ \overline{b} \\  
    e, f ‚àà \Exp 
        & ‚âú p ‚àà K ‚à£ b ‚àà \Bool ‚à£ e +_b f ‚à£ e ; f ‚à£ e^{(b)} 
\end{align*}
where \(e +_b f\) is the if statement with condition \(b\); \(e;f\) is the sequencing of expression \(e\) and \(f\); finally \(e^{(b)}\) is the while loop with body \(e\) and condition \(b\).
A GKAT expression can be unfolded into a KAT expression in the usual manner~\cite{kozen_KleeneAlgebraTests_1997c}:
\begin{align*}
    e +_b f & ‚âú b; e + \overline{b}; f &
    e^{(b)} & ‚âú (b; e)^*; \overline{b}.
\end{align*}
Then the semantics of each expression \(‚ü¶e‚üß\) can be computed by the semantics of Kleene Algebra with tests~\cite{kozen_KleeneAlgebraTests_1997c}.
An important construct in the semantics is \emph{atoms}, which are a string of all the primitive tests either in its positive or negative form: for \(B ‚âú \{b‚ÇÅ, b‚ÇÇ, ‚Ä¶, b‚Çô\}\)
\[\At_B ‚âú \{b‚ÇÅ' ‚ãÖ b‚ÇÇ' ‚ãÖ ‚ãØ ‚ãÖ b‚Çô' ‚à£ b·µ¢' ‚àà \{b·µ¢, \overline{b·µ¢}\}\}.\]
Follow the conventional notation, we denote an atom using \(Œ±, Œ≤\); and we sometimes omit the subscript \(B\) when no confusing can arise.
Intuitively, atoms are truth assignments to each primitive tests, indicating which primitive tests is satisfied in the current program states.

For the sake of brevity, we omit the complete definition of GKAT and KAT semantics, we refer the reader to previous works~\cite{smolka_GuardedKleeneAlgebra_2020,schmid_GuardedKleeneAlgebra_2021,kozen_KleeneAlgebraTests_1997c}, which explains these semantics in detail.
Our work avoids directly interact with the semantics by leveraging results in the coalgebraic theory of GKAT, which we will recap below.

Formally, GKAT coalgebras over primitive actions \(K\) and primitive tests \(T\) are coalgebras over the following functor:
\[G(S) ‚âú (\{\accept, \reject\} + S √ó K)^{\At_B}.\] 
Intuitively, given a state \(s ‚àà S\) and an atom \(Œ± ‚àà \At\), \(Œ¥(s, Œ±)\) will deterministically execute one of the following: reject \(Œ±\), denoted as \(Œ¥(s, Œ±) = \reject\); accept \(Œ±\), denoted \(Œ¥(s, Œ±) = \accept\); or transition to a state \(s' ‚àà S\) and execute action \(p ‚àà K\), denoted as \(Œ¥(s, Œ±) = (s', p)\).

This deterministic behavior contrast that of Kleene coalgebra with tests~\cite{kozen_CoalgebraicTheoryKleene_2017}, where for each atom, the state can accept or  reject the atom (but not both), yet the state can also non-deterministically transition to multiple different state via the same atom, while executing different actions.
% TODO: I think this belongs in the intro
As we will see later, the deterministic behavior of GKAT coalgebra not only enables a more versatile symbolic algorithm than KCT~\cite{pous_SymbolicAlgorithmsLanguage_2015}, but also present challenges. 
Specifically, GKAT coalgebra requires normalization to compute finite trace equivalences~\cite{smolka_GuardedKleeneAlgebra_2020}, where we will remove all the state that cannot lead to acceptance, which are called ``dead states''. 

In previous works, these dead states are detected after all the necessary state and transition is computed.
This approach requires storing all the information of the coalgebra in memory, which do not allow on-the-fly computation, which can not only terminate whenever a counter-example is found, but can also erase past states from memory. 

However, to truly understand the on-the-fly algorithm, we will first need to define ``dead states'', and its role in defining the coalgebraic semantic of GKAT. 

\subsection{Liveness and Sub-GKAT coalgebras}

% liveness

Traditionally, live and dead states are defined by whether they can reach an accepting state~\cite{smolka_GuardedKleeneAlgebra_2020}. However, it is straightforward to show that principle sub-coalgebra \(‚ü®s‚ü©_S\) exactly correspondents to reachable states of \(s\) in coalgebra \(S\). 
Thus, the classical definition is equivalent to the following:
\begin{definition}[liveness of states]\label{def:liveness-of-states}
    A state \(s\) is \emph{accepting} if there exists a \(Œ± ‚àà \At\) s.t. \(Œ¥(s, Œ±) = \accept\). A state \(s'\) is \emph{live} if there exists an accepting state \(s' ‚àà ‚ü®s‚ü©\). A state \(s'\) is \emph{dead} if there is no accepting state in \(‚ü®s‚ü©\).
\end{definition}
This alternative liveness definition can help us formally prove important theorems regarding reachability and liveness without performing induction on traces. We can show the following lemmas as examples:
\begin{lemma}\label{thm:dead-iff-all-reachable-dead}
    A state \(s\) is dead if and only if all elements in \(‚ü®s‚ü©\) is dead.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    \(‚ü∏\) direction is true, because \(s ‚àà ‚ü®s‚ü©\): if all \(‚ü®s‚ü©\) is dead, then \(s\) is dead. 
    \(‚üπ\) direction can be proven as follows.
    Take \(s' ‚àà ‚ü®s‚ü©\), then \(‚ü®s'‚ü© ‚äë ‚ü®s‚ü©\) by definition. 
    Since there is no accepting state in \(‚ü®s‚ü©\), thus there cannot be any accepting state in \(‚ü®s'‚ü©\), hence \(‚ü®s'‚ü©\) is also dead.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[homomorphism perserves liveness]\label{thm:hom-preserve-liveness}
    Given a homomorphism \(h: S ‚Üí U\) and a state \(s ‚àà S\):
    \[\text{\(s\) is live} ‚ü∫ \text{\(h(s)\) is live}\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    Because homomorphic image preserves principle sub-GKAT coalgebra (\Cref{thm:homo-img-preserve-principle-sub-coalg})
    \[h(‚ü®s‚ü©_S) = ‚ü®h(s)‚ü©_U;\]
    therefore for any state \(s' ‚àà S\):
    \[s' ‚àà ‚ü®s‚ü©_S ‚ü∫ h(s') ‚àà h(‚ü®s‚ü©_S) ‚ü∫ h(s') ‚àà ‚ü®h(s)‚ü©_U.\]
    And because \(s'\) is accepting if and only if \(h(s')\) accepting by definition of homomorphism; then \(‚ü®s‚ü©_S\) contains an accepting state if and only if \(h(‚ü®s‚ü©_S) = ‚ü®h(s)‚ü©_U\) contains an accepting state. 
    Therefore, \(s\) is live in \(S\) if and only if \(h(s)\) is live in \(U\).
\end{proof}

The above theorem then leads to several interesting liveness preservation properties for important structures on coalgebras, like sub-coalgebra and bisimulation.

\begin{corollary}[sub-coalgebra perserves liveness]\label{thm:sub-coalg-preserve-liveness}
    For a sub-coalgebra \(S' ‚äë S\) and a state \(s ‚àà S'\), \(s\) is live in \(S'\) if and only if \(s\) is live in \(S\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}

\subsection{Normalization And Semantics}

(Possibly infinite) trace model \(ùí¢_œâ\) is the final coalgebra of GKAT coalgebras~\cite{schmid_GuardedKleeneAlgebra_2021}.
The finality of the model implies that every state in any GKAT coalgebra \(S\) can be assigned a semantics under the unique homomorphism \(‚ü¶-‚üß^{œâ}_{S}: S ‚Üí ùí¢_œâ\); and such semantic equivalences can indeed be identified by bisimulation~\cite{schmid_GuardedKleeneAlgebra_2021}:
\(‚ü¶s‚üß^{œâ}_{S} = ‚ü¶t‚üß^{œâ}_{T}\) if and only if there exists a bisimulation \({‚àº} ‚äÜ S √ó T\), s.t. \(s ‚àº t\).

The infinite trace equivalences can be directly computed with bisimulation on derivative, which supports on-the-fly algorithm as demonstrated by similar systems~\cite{kozen_CoalgebraicTheoryKleene_2017,almeida_DecidingKATHoare_2012,pous_SymbolicAlgorithmsLanguage_2015}. 
However, the \emph{finite} trace model \(ùí¢\) is the final coalgebra of GKAT coalgebras without dead states, which we call \emph{normal GKAT coalgebra}~\cite{smolka_GuardedKleeneAlgebra_2020}. 
Fortunately every GKAT coalgebra can be normalized by rerouting all the transition from dead states to rejection.
Concretely, given a GKAT coalgebra \(S ‚âú (S, Œ¥_S)\), we let \(Œ¥_{\norm(S)} : S ‚Üí G(S)\) is defined as \(Œ¥_{\norm(S)}(s, Œ±) ‚âú \reject\) when \(Œ¥_S(s, Œ±) = (s', p)\) and \(s'\) is dead in \(S\); and \(Œ¥_{\norm(S)}(s, Œ±) ‚âú Œ¥_S(s, Œ±)\) otherwise; then \((S, Œ¥_{\norm(S)})\) is the \emph{normalized} coalgebra of \(S ‚âú (S, Œ¥_S)\) denoted as \(\norm(S)\).

The finality of \(ùí¢\) means that the finite trace semantics \(‚ü¶-‚üß\) is the unique coalgebra homomorphism \(\norm(S) ‚Üí ùí¢\). 
Furthermore, the finite trace equivalence between \(s ‚àà S\) and \(u ‚àà U\) can be computed by first normalizing \(S\) and \(U\), then decide whether there is a bisimulation on \(\norm(S)\) and \(\norm(U)\) that includes \((s, t)\).
For a more detailed explanation on the finite trace semantics, we refer the reader to the work of \Citeauthor{smolka_GuardedKleeneAlgebra_2020}~\cite{smolka_GuardedKleeneAlgebra_2020}.

Besides giving us the finite-trace semantics, the normalization operation also connects the finite and infinite trace semantics, because it is an endofunctor on the category of GKAT coalgebra.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:norm-functor}
    \(\norm\) is an endofunctor in the category GKAT coalgebra.
    More specifically, if \(h: S ‚Üí U\) is a GKAT homomorphism, then \(h: \norm(S) ‚Üí \norm(U)\) is also a homomorphism.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    Recall that \(h\) is a homomorphism if and only if for all \(s ‚àà S\) and \(Œ± ‚àà \At\):
    \begin{itemize}[nosep]
        \item for a result \(r ‚àà \{\reject, \accept\}\), 
        \[Œ¥_S(s, Œ±) = r ‚ü∫ Œ¥_U(h(s), Œ±) = r;\]
        \item for any \(s' ‚àà S\) and \(p ‚àà K\), 
        \[Œ¥_S(s, Œ±) = (s', p) ‚ü∫ Œ¥_{U}(h(s), Œ±) = (h(s'), p).\]
    \end{itemize}

    Then we show that \(h: \norm(S) ‚Üí \norm(U)\) is a homomorphism, this is a consequence of homomorphism preserves liveness (\Cref{thm:hom-preserve-liveness}): for all \(s ‚àà \norm(S)\) and \(Œ± ‚àà \At\):
    \begin{align*}
        & Œ¥_{\norm(S)}(s, Œ±) = \accept \\*
        ‚ü∫{}& Œ¥_S(s, Œ±) = \accept \\*  
        ‚ü∫{}& Œ¥_U(h(s), Œ±) = \accept \\*  
        ‚ü∫{}& Œ¥_{\norm(U)}(h(s), Œ±) = \accept;\\
        & Œ¥_{\norm(S)}(s, Œ±) = \reject \\*  
        ‚ü∫{}& Œ¥_{S}(s, Œ±) = \reject 
        \text{ or } Œ¥_{S}(s, Œ±) = (s', p), s' \text{ is dead} \\*
        ‚ü∫{}& Œ¥_{U}(h(s), Œ±) = \reject \\*
        & \text{ or } Œ¥_{U}(h(s), Œ±) = (h(s'), p), h(s') \text{ is dead} \\*  
        ‚ü∫{}& Œ¥_{\norm(U)}(h(s), Œ±) = \reject;\\
        & Œ¥_{\norm(S)}(s, Œ±) = (s', p) \\*
        ‚ü∫{}& Œ¥_{S}(s, Œ±) = (s', p), s' \text{ is live} \\*  
        ‚ü∫{}& Œ¥_{U}(h(s), Œ±) = (h(s'), p), h(s') \text{ is live}\\* 
        ‚ü∫{}& Œ¥_{\norm(U)}(h(s), Œ±) = (h(s'), p).
        \qedhere
    \end{align*}
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{thm:norm-sub-coalg}
    Normalization preserves sub-coalgebra, i.e. if \(S' ‚äë S\) then \(\norm(S') ‚äë \norm(S)\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
    By letting the homomorphism in~\Cref{thm:norm-functor} to be the inclusion homomorphism \(i: S' ‚Üí S\)
\end{proof}

Because of the functoriality, we can show that two states are infinite-trace equivalent implies these two states are finite-trance equivalent.
This gives us more tool in proving semantic equivalence between two states in GKAT coalgebras: proving bisimulation in the GKAT coalgebra is already enough to obtain the semantic equivalence for two states.

\begin{corollary}\label{thm:inf-trace-equiv-implies-fin-trace-equiv}
    Given two states in two GKAT coalgebra \(s ‚àà S, u ‚àà U\), \(‚ü¶s‚üß^{œâ}_{S} = ‚ü¶u‚üß^{œâ}_{U} ‚üπ ‚ü¶s‚üß_{S} = ‚ü¶u‚üß_{U}\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
    Because \(‚ü¶s‚üß^{œâ}_{S} = ‚ü¶u‚üß^{œâ}_{U}\), there exists a bisimulation \({‚àº} ‚äÜ S √ó U\) s.t. \(s ‚àº u\)~\cite{schmid_GuardedKleeneAlgebra_2021}.
    Therefore, we have the following span in the category of GKAT coalgebra:
    \[\begin{tikzcd}
        S & ‚àº \ar{r}{œÄ‚ÇÇ} \ar[swap]{l}{œÄ‚ÇÅ} & U
    \end{tikzcd}\]
    Then by~\Cref{thm:norm-functor}, \(\norm(‚àº)\) is a bisimulation between \(\norm(S)\) and \(\norm(U)\):
    \[\begin{tikzcd}
        \norm(S) 
        & \norm(‚àº) \ar{r}{œÄ‚ÇÇ} \ar[swap]{l}{œÄ‚ÇÅ} 
        & \norm(U)
    \end{tikzcd}\]
    And because normalization operation preserves states in \(‚àº\), therefore \((s, u) ‚àà \norm(‚àº)\), and because \(\norm(‚àº)\) is a bisimulation between the normalization of \(S\) and \(U\), therefore \(‚ü¶s‚üß_{S} = ‚ü¶u‚üß_{U}\)
\end{proof}

\section{On-The-Fly Bisimulation}

The original algorithm for deciding GKAT equivalences~\cite{smolka_GuardedKleeneAlgebra_2020} requires the entire automaton to be known prior to the execution of the bisimulation algorithm; specifically, in order to compute the liveness of a state \(s\), it is necessary iterate through all its reachable states \(‚ü®s‚ü©\) to see if there are any accepting states within.
This limitation poses challenges to design an efficient on-the-fly algorithm for GKAT.
In order to make the decision procedure scalable, we will need to merge the normalization and bisimulation procedure, so that our algorithm can normalized the automaton only when we need to.

In this section, we introduce an algorithm that merges bisimulation and normalization where we only need to test the liveness of the state when a disparity in the bisimulation has been found.
For example, when one automaton leads to reject where the other transition to a state, then we will need to verify whether that state is dead or not.

This on-the-fly algorithm inherits the efficiency of the original algorithm~\cite{smolka_GuardedKleeneAlgebra_2020}, where the worst case will require two passes of the automaton, where one pass will try to establish a bisimulation, when failed the other pass will kick in and compute whether the failed states are dead.
In some special case, the on-the-fly algorithm can even out perform the original algorithm; for example, when the two input automata are bisimular (even when they are not normal), the on-the-fly algorithm can skip the liveness checking, only performing the bisimulation.

\begin{theorem}[sub-coalgebras perserve and reflect bisimulation]\label{thm:sub-coalg-preserve-bisim}
    Given any sub-coalgebra \(S' ‚äë S\) and \(T' ‚äë T\),
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Given a bisimulation \(‚àº\) between \(S'\) and \(T'\), then \(‚àº\) is also a bisimulation between \(S\) and \(T\);
        \item if there exists a bisimulation \(‚àº\) between \(S\) and \(T\), then the restriction 
        \begin{mathpar}
            ‚àº_{S', T'} ‚âú \{(s, t) ‚à£ s ‚àà S', t ‚àà T', s ‚àº t\}

        forms a bisimulation between \(S'\) and \(T'\).
    \end{itemize}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    To prove that bisimulation \(‚àº\) between \(S'\) and \(T'\) is also a bisimulation of \(S\) and \(T\), we can simply enlarge the diagram by the inclusion homomorphism
    \[

            & ‚àº \ar[swap]{l}{œÄ‚ÇÅ} \ar{r}{œÄ‚ÇÇ} \ar{d}{Œ¥_‚àº}

            & G(‚àº) \ar[swap]{l}{G(œÄ‚ÇÅ)} \ar{r}{G(œÄ‚ÇÇ)} & T' \ar[hook]{r}{G(i)} & T \\  
        \end{tikzcd}

    Because the inclusion homomorphism \(i\) doesn't change the input thus, we have:
    \begin{mathpar}
        {‚àº} \xrightarrow{œÄ‚ÇÅ} S' \xrightarrow{i} S = {‚àº} \xrightarrow{œÄ‚ÇÅ} S \and 
        {‚àº} \xrightarrow{œÄ‚ÇÇ} T' \xrightarrow{i} T = {‚àº} \xrightarrow{œÄ‚ÇÇ} T
    \end{mathpar}

    To prove that the bisimulation can be restricted, we first realize that \(‚àº_{S', T'}\) is a pre-image of the maximal bisimulation \(‚â£_{S', T'}\) along the inclusion homomorphism \(i: {‚àº} ‚Üí {‚â°_{S, T}}\).

    \[

\end{proof}

\Cref{thm:sub-coalg-preserve-bisim} allows us to only search for bisimulation on a sub-coalgebra, speeding up our search algorithm.
Another way to speed up the algorithm is to use efficient data structures to find a bisimulation equivalence instead of a bisimulation relation.
This optimization is a special case of the \emph{up-to technique}~\cite{bonchi_GeneralAccountCoinduction_2017a}.
Specifically, we will extend~\Cref{thm:bisim-iff-bisim-eq} to a setting where the bisimulation is no longer over the same coalgebra.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:GKAT-bisim-iff-bisim-eq}
    Given two states in two coalgebras \(s ‚àà S, u ‚àà U\), let \(S + U\) be the coproduct coalgebra of \(S\) and \(U\)~\cite{rutten_UniversalCoalgebraTheory_2000}. 
    There exists a bisimulation \({‚àº} ‚äÜ S √ó U\) s.t. \(s ‚àº u\) if and only if there exists a bisimulation equivalence on \({‚âÉ} ‚äÜ (S + U) √ó (S + U)\), s.t. \(s ‚âÉ u\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    Notice that both \(S\) and \(U\) are sub-coalgebra of \(S + U\) witnessed by the canonical injection \(\mathrm{inj}‚Çó: S ‚Üí S + U\) and \(\mathrm{inj}·µ£: U ‚Üí S + U\).

    Let \(‚àº\) be a bisimulation and \(‚âÉ\) be a bisimulation equivalence, by~\Cref{thm:sub-coalg-preserve-bisim} and~\Cref{thm:bisim-iff-bisim-eq}:
    \begin{align*}
        & ‚àÉ~ {‚àº} ‚äÜ S √ó U, s ‚àº u \\
        ‚ü∫{}& ‚àÉ~ {‚àº} ‚äÜ (S+U) √ó (S+U), s ‚àº u \\  
        ‚ü∫{}& ‚àÉ~ {‚âÉ} ‚äÜ (S+U) √ó (S+U), s ‚âÉ u.
        \qedhere
    \end{align*}
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Recursive Construction]\label{thm:recursive-construction}
    Given two GKAT coalgebra \(S\) and \(U\), and two of their elements \(s ‚àà S\) and \(u ‚àà U\),
    there exists a bisimulation \({‚àº} ‚äÜ \norm(‚ü®s‚ü©) √ó \norm(‚ü®u‚ü©)\) s.t. \(s ‚àº u\), if and only if all the following holds:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item\label{itm:acc-condition} for all \(Œ± ‚àà \At\), \(Œ¥_{S}(s, Œ±) = \accept ‚ü∫ Œ¥_{U}(u, Œ±) = \accept\);
        \item\label{itm:rej-or-dead} \(s\) reject \(Œ±\) or transition to a dead state via \(Œ±\) if and only if \(u\) rejects \(Œ±\) or transition to a dead state via \(Œ±\);  
        \item\label{itm:transition-bisim} If \(Œ¥_{S}(s, Œ±) = (s', p)\) and \(Œ¥_{U}(u, Œ±) = (u', p)\), then there exists a bisimulation \({‚àº'} ‚äÜ \norm(‚ü®s'‚ü©) √ó \norm(‚ü®u'‚ü©)\), s.t. \(s' ‚àº' u'\);
        \item\label{itm:transition-dead} If \(Œ¥_{S}(s, Œ±) = (s', p)\) and \(Œ¥_{U}(u, Œ±) = (u', q)\), s.t. \(p ‚â† q\), then both \(s'\) and \(t'\) are dead.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    We first prove \(‚üπ\) direction, recall that there exists a bisimulation \({‚àº} ‚äÜ \norm(‚ü®s‚ü©) √ó \norm(‚ü®u‚ü©)\) if and only if for all \(s‚ÇÅ ‚àº u‚ÇÅ\):
    \begin{itemize}
        \item for all results \(r ‚àà \{\accept, \reject\}\): \(Œ¥_{\norm(S)}(s‚ÇÅ, Œ±) = r ‚ü∫ Œ¥_{\norm(U)}(u‚ÇÇ, Œ±) = r\);
        \item otherwise, let \((s‚ÇÇ, p) ‚âú Œ¥_{\norm(S)}(s‚ÇÅ, Œ±)\) and \((u‚ÇÇ, q) ‚âú Œ¥_{\norm(u)}(u‚ÇÅ, Œ±)\), then \(p = q\) and \(s‚ÇÇ ‚àº u‚ÇÇ\)
    \end{itemize}

    The condition \labelcref{itm:acc-condition} holds:
    \begin{align*}
        Œ¥_S(s, Œ±) = \accept 
        ‚ü∫{}& Œ¥_{\norm(S)}(s, Œ±) = \accept \\
        ‚ü∫{}& Œ¥_‚àº((s,u), Œ±) = \accept \\ 
        ‚ü∫{}& Œ¥_{\norm(U)}(u, Œ±) = \accept \\
        ‚ü∫{}& Œ¥_U(u, Œ±) = \accept
    \end{align*}

    The condition~\labelcref{itm:rej-or-dead} holds: 
    \begin{align*}
        & Œ¥_S(s, Œ±) \text{ rejects or transition to dead states} \\
        ‚ü∫{}& Œ¥_{\norm(S)}(s, Œ±) = \reject \\
        ‚ü∫{}& Œ¥_{\norm(U)}(u, Œ±) = \reject \\
        ‚ü∫{}& Œ¥_U(u, Œ±) \text{ rejects or transition to dead states}.
    \end{align*}

    The condition~\labelcref{itm:transition-bisim} holds, by case analysis on the liveness of \(s'\) and \(u'\).
    First note that \(s'\) and \(u'\) has to be both live or both dead: because \(Œ¥_S(s, Œ±) = (s', p)\), then \(\norm(Œ¥_S)(s', Œ±)\) can either be rejection or \((s',p)\), and so is \(\norm(Œ¥_U)(u', Œ±)\):
    \begin{align*}
        s' \text{ is live} 
        & ‚ü∫ Œ¥_{\norm(S)}(s, Œ±) = (s', p) \\
        & ‚ü∫ Œ¥_{\norm(U)}(u, Œ±) = (u', p) \\
        & ‚ü∫ u' \text{ is live}.
    \end{align*}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item If both \(s'\) and \(u'\) are live, then \(s' ‚àº u'\). By~\cref{thm:sub-coalg-preserve-bisim}, the bisimulation \(‚àº'\) is just \(‚àº\) restricted to \(‚ü®s'‚ü©\) and \(‚ü®u'‚ü©\).
        \item If both \(s'\) and \(u'\) are dead, then \(‚àº'\) can just be the singleton relation, according to~\cref{thm:bisim-between-dead}.
    \end{itemize}

    The condition~\labelcref{itm:transition-dead} holds: by the proof of condition~\labelcref{itm:transition-bisim}, \(s'\) and \(u'\) has to be either both live or both dead; if they are both live, then there cannot be a element in \(G(‚àº)\) that can project to \((s', p)\) under \(œÄ‚ÇÅ\) but projects to \((t', q)\) under \(œÄ‚ÇÇ\). Thus both \(s'\) and \(t'\) has to be dead.

    We then show the \(‚ü∏\) direction, for arbitrary \(s' ‚àà S\) and \(u' ‚àà U\), we use \(‚â°_{s', u'}\) to denote the maximal bisimulation between \(\norm(‚ü®s'‚ü©)\) and \(\norm(‚ü®u'‚ü©)\).
    \begin{align*}
        {‚àº'} ‚âú ‚ãÉ \{‚â°_{s', u'} & ‚à£ ‚àÉ Œ± ‚àà \At, p ‚àà K, \\*
            & Œ¥_{\norm(S)}(s, Œ±) = (s', p) \\*
            & \text{ and } Œ¥_{\norm(U)}(u, Œ±) = (u', p)\}.
    \end{align*}
    For all the \(s'\) and \(u'\) in the above definition, \(‚ü®s'‚ü© ‚äë ‚ü®s‚ü©\) and \(‚ü®u'‚ü© ‚äë ‚ü®u‚ü©\), therefore by~\Cref{thm:norm-sub-coalg}, \(\norm(‚ü®s'‚ü©) ‚äë \norm(‚ü®s‚ü©)\) and \(\norm(‚ü®u'‚ü©) ‚äë \norm(‚ü®u‚ü©)\). 
    By~\Cref{thm:sub-coalg-preserve-bisim}, every \(‚â°_{s', u'}\) is a bisimulation between \(\norm(‚ü®s‚ü©)\) and \(\norm(‚ü®t‚ü©)\), and because bisimulation is closed under arbitrary union~\cite{rutten_UniversalCoalgebraTheory_2000}, \(‚àº'\) is a bisimulation between \(\norm(‚ü®s‚ü©)\) and \(\norm(‚ü®t‚ü©)\).

    To obtain the desired bisimulation \({‚àº}\) between \(\norm(‚ü®s‚ü©)\) and \(\norm(‚ü®u‚ü©)\), we add the pair \((s, t)\) to \(‚àº'\), 
    \[{‚àº} ‚âú {‚àº'} ‚à™ \{(s, u)\},\] 
    with the following transition \(Œ¥_{‚àº}\): for all \(Œ± ‚àà \At\),
    \begin{itemize}[nosep]
        \item if \(Œ¥_{\norm(S)}(s, Œ±) = Œ¥_{\norm(U)}(u, Œ±) = \accept\), then \(Œ¥_{‚àº}((s, u), Œ±) ‚âú \accept\);
        \item if \(Œ¥_{\norm(S)}(s, Œ±) = Œ¥_{\norm(U)}(u, Œ±) = \reject\), then \(Œ¥_{‚àº}((s, u), Œ±) ‚âú \reject\);
        \item if \(Œ¥_{\norm(S)}(s, Œ±) = (s', p)\) and \(Œ¥_{\norm(U)}(u, Œ±) = (u', p)\), then \(Œ¥_{‚àº}((s, u), Œ±) = ((s', u'), p)\);
        \item for all \((s', u') ‚àà {‚àº}'\) that is not equal to \((s, u)\), we let \(Œ¥_‚àº\) inherits the transition of \(Œ¥_{‚àº'}\), i.e. \(Œ¥_{‚àº}((s', u'), Œ±) = Œ¥_{‚àº'}((s', u'), Œ±)\)
    \end{itemize}
    \emph{if \(Œ¥_{‚àº}\) is well-defined}, then we can verify that \(‚àº\) is indeed a bisimulation between \(\norm(‚ü®s‚ü©)\) and \(\norm(‚ü®u‚ü©)\) where \(s ‚àº u\). 
    We show the slightly more complicated case: \(Œ¥_{\norm(S)}(s, Œ±) = (s', p)\) and \(Œ¥_{\norm(U)}(u, Œ±) = (u', p)\) implies \(s' ‚àº u'\) as an example. 
    By condition~\labelcref{itm:transition-bisim}, there exists a bisimulation \(‚àº_{s', u'} ‚äÜ \norm(‚ü®s'‚ü©) √ó \norm(‚ü®u'‚ü©)\), and because \(‚â°_{s', u'}\) is the maximal bisimulation between \(\norm(‚ü®s'‚ü©)\) and \(\norm(‚ü®u'‚ü©)\),
    \[(s', u') ‚àà {‚àº_{s', u'}} ‚äÜ {‚â°_{s', u'}} ‚äÜ {‚àº}.\]

    Finally, we demonstrate that \(Œ¥_‚àº\) is well-defined by leveraging the conditions in~\Cref{thm:recursive-construction}. 
    Specifically, we will show that the definition of \(Œ¥_‚àº\) covers all the possible cases, by case analysis on the result of \(Œ¥_S\): for all \(Œ± ‚àà \At\),
    \begin{itemize}
        \item If \(Œ¥_S(s, Œ±) = \accept\), then by condition~\labelcref{itm:acc-condition}, \(Œ¥_U(u, Œ±) = \accept\); therefore \[(Œ¥_{\norm(S)})(s, Œ±) = \norm(Œ¥_{\norm(U)})(u, Œ±) = \accept.\]
        \item If \(Œ¥_S(s, Œ±)\) transitions to a dead state or reject, then by condition~\labelcref{itm:rej-or-dead} \(Œ¥_U(u, Œ±)\) will also transition to a dead state or reject, then \[Œ¥_{\norm(S)}(s, Œ±) = Œ¥_{\norm(U)}(u, Œ±) = \reject.\]
        \item If \(Œ¥_S(s, Œ±) = (s', p)\) and \(s'\) is live, then \(Œ¥_U(u, Œ±) = (u', p)\) necessarily holds, otherwise it would violate one of conditions~\labelcref{itm:acc-condition,itm:rej-or-dead,itm:transition-dead}. 

        By condition~\labelcref{itm:transition-bisim}, there exists a bisimulation \(‚àº_{s', u'}\) between \(\norm(‚ü®s'‚ü©)\) and \(\norm(‚ü®u'‚ü©)\) s.t. \(s' ‚àº_{s', u'} u'\). Because bisimulation preserves liveness (\Cref{thm:bisim-preserve-liveness}), \(s', u'\) has to be both dead or live. 
        Finally, because \(s'\) is live, therefore \(u'\) is also live, and we obtain the final case in the definition of \(Œ¥_{‚àº}\): \(Œ¥_{\norm(S)}(s, Œ±) = (s', p)\) and \(Œ¥_{\norm(U)}(u, Œ±) = (u', p)\).
        \qedhere
    \end{itemize}
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Correctness]\label{thm:recursive-construction-correctness}
    For any two states in two GKAT coalgebra \(s ‚àà S, u ‚àà U\), \(s\) and \(u\) are finite-trace equivalent if and only if there exists a \emph{bisimulation equivalence} \(‚âÉ\) in \(\norm(‚ü®s‚ü©) + \norm(‚ü®u‚ü©)\), which is equivalent to all the following conditions hold:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item for all \(Œ± ‚àà \At\), \(Œ¥_{S}(s, Œ±) = \accept ‚ü∫ Œ¥_{U}(u, Œ±) = \accept\);
        \item \(s\) reject \(Œ±\) or transition to a dead state via \(Œ±\) if and only if \(u\) rejects \(Œ±\) or transition to a dead state via \(Œ±\);  
        \item if \(Œ¥_{S}(s, Œ±) = (s', p)\) and \(Œ¥_{U}(u, Œ±) = (u', p)\), then there exists a bisimulation equivalence \({‚âÉ'}\) in \(\norm(‚ü®s'‚ü©) + \norm(‚ü®u'‚ü©)\), s.t. \(s' ‚âÉ' u'\);
        \item if \(Œ¥_{S}(s, Œ±) = (s', p)\) and \(Œ¥_{U}(u, Œ±) = (u', q)\), s.t. \(p ‚â† q\), then both \(s'\) and \(t'\) are dead.
    \end{enumerate}
    Notice that above condition is similar to those in~\cref{thm:recursive-construction}, except bisimulation is replaced with bisimulation \emph{equivalence}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
    By the standard argument with normalization preserves sub-coalgebra (\Cref{thm:norm-sub-coalg}), we can obtain 
    \(\norm(‚ü®s‚ü©) ‚äë \norm(S)\) and \(\norm(‚ü®u‚ü©) ‚äë \norm(U)\).
    Because sub-coalgebra reflects and preserves bisimulation (\Cref{thm:sub-coalg-preserve-bisim}) and the correspondence between bisimulations and bisimulation equivalences in GKAT (\cref{thm:GKAT-bisim-iff-bisim-eq}): let \({‚àº_{s, u}} ‚äÜ \norm(‚ü®s‚ü©) √ó \norm(‚ü®u‚ü©)\) and \({‚àº} ‚äÜ \norm(S) √ó \norm(U)\) be bisimulations and \(‚âÉ\) be a bisimulation equivalence in \(\norm(‚ü®s‚ü©) + \norm(‚ü®u‚ü©)\):
    \begin{align*}
        & \text{Conditions in \cref{thm:recursive-construction} is satisfied} \\  
        ‚ü∫{}& \text{Conditions in current theorem is satisfied}\\
        ‚ü∫{}& ‚àÉ~ {‚âÉ}, s ‚âÉ u 
        ‚ü∫ ‚àÉ~ {‚àº_{s, u}}, s ‚àº u \\
        ‚ü∫{}& ‚àÉ~ {‚àº} , s ‚àº u 
        ‚ü∫ ‚ü¶s‚üß_{S} = ‚ü¶u‚üß_{U}. \qedhere
    \end{align*}
\end{proof}

The above theorem already gives us an algorithm to recursively decide whether \(‚ü¶s‚üß_{S} = ‚ü¶u‚üß_U\), when \(‚ü®s‚ü©_S\) and \(‚ü®u‚ü©_U\) is finite.
However, this algorithm can be further optimized in several ways: we will then derive that a dead state can never relate to live states. This means that when checking the bisimulation of states \(s\) and \(t\), if we already know one of them is dead, we only need to check whether the other is dead, instead of going through all the conditions in~\cref{thm:recursive-construction}.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:bisim-one-dead}
    Given two states \(s ‚àà S\) and \(t ‚àà T\), if \(s\) is a dead state in \(S\), then there exists a bisimulation equivalence \(‚âÉ\) in \(S + T\) where \(s ‚âÉ t\) if and only if \(t\) is dead. Similarly, for \(t ‚àà T\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    Notice because of the homomorphism \(\mathrm{inj_l}: S ‚Üí S + T\) and homomorphism preserves liveness (\cref{thm:hom-preserve-liveness}), therefore \(s ‚àà S\) is dead if and only if \(s ‚àà S + T\) is dead; similarly for \(t ‚àà T\).

    If there exists a bisimulation equivalence \(‚âÉ\), s.t. \(s ‚âÉ t\), because \(s\) is dead and bisimulation preserves liveness~\cref{thm:bisim-preserve-liveness}, then \(t\) is dead. 

    And if both \(t\) and \(s\) is dead, then a bisimulation between \(S\) and \(T\) can be constructed in~\cref{thm:bisim-between-dead}, which induces a bisimulation equivalence via~\Cref{thm:GKAT-bisim-iff-bisim-eq}.
\end{proof}

\section{Symbolic Algorithm}

Given the alphabet \(K, B\), a \emph{symbolic GKAT coalgebra} \(SÃÇ ‚âú ‚ü®S, œµÃÇ, Œ¥ÃÇ‚ü©\) consists of a state set \(S\) and a accepting function \(œµÃÇ\) and a transition function \(Œ¥ÃÇ\):
\begin{mathpar}
    œµÃÇ: S ‚Üí ùí´(\Bool_B), \and
    Œ¥ÃÇ: S ‚Üí ùí´(\Bool_B √ó S √ó K),
\end{mathpar}
where \(\Bool_B\) is the free boolean algebra over \(B\) (boolean expressions modulo boolean algebra axioms); for all states \(s ‚àà S\), all the booleans are ``disjoint''; namely the conjunction of any two expression from the set \(\{œµÃÇ(s)\} ‚à™ \{b ‚à£ ‚àÉ (b, s', p) ‚àà Œ¥(s)\}\) are false. 
We will then use \(œÅÃÇ(s): \Bool_B\) to denote the boolean expressions that contain all the atoms that the state \(s\) rejects, and \(œÅÃÇ(s)\) can be computed as follows:
\[œÅÃÇ(s) ‚âú ¬¨ œµÃÇ(s) ‚à® ¬¨ \left( ‚ãÅ_{(b, s', p) ‚àà Œ¥(s)} b \right)\]

Instead of modeling each atom individually in the automata, we group them into boolean expressions, this leads to a much more space efficient automata, and enables efficient bisimulation algorithms using off-the-shelf SAT solvers.

With the above intuition in mind, a symbolic GKAT coalgebra \(SÃÇ ‚âú ‚ü®S, œµÃÇ, Œ¥ÃÇ‚ü©\) can be lowered into a GKAT coalgebra \(‚ü®S, Œ¥‚ü©\) in the following manner:
\begin{align}\label{cons:lowering}
Œ¥(s, Œ±) ‚âú \begin{cases}
    \accept & ‚àÉ b ‚àà œµÃÇ(s), Œ± ‚â§ b \\  
    (s', p) & ‚àÉ b ‚àà \Bool_B, Œ± ‚â§ b \text{ and } Œ¥(s, b) = (s', p) \\  
    \reject & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{align}
This is well-defined, i.e. no more than one clause can be satisfied precisely because the boolean expressions appear in \(œµÃÇ\) and \(Œ¥ÃÇ\) are disjoint.
The trace semantics of a GKAT coalgebra \(‚ü®S, œµÃÇ, Œ¥ÃÇ‚ü©\) is then defined as the trace semantics of its lowering \(‚ü®S, Œ¥‚ü©\).

    \[{Œ¥ÃÇ}‚ÇÅ(*) ‚âú \{b ‚Ü¶ (*, p), ¬¨ b ‚Ü¶ (*, p)\} \qquad 
    {Œ¥ÃÇ}‚ÇÇ(*) ‚âú \{‚ä§ ‚Ü¶ (*, p)\},\]

We would like to explore several desirable theoretical properties of both derivatives and Thompson's construction.
Specifically, the correctness, i.e. the semantics of the ``start state'' the both construction have the same preserves the trace semantics of the expression; finiteness, i.e. the coalgebra generated is always finite, which means that our equivalence algorithm will eventually terminate; and finally, how does the number of reachable state relate to the size of the expression, so that we can estimate the complexity of the equivalence checking algorithm.
